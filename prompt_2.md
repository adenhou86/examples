Two diagrams: first the system contract, then a row per query type.How to read the contract: each node in the DAG is essentially `(tool, args, depends_on)`, where `args` may contain placeholders like `{n2.answer}` that the executor resolves at dispatch time from `answer_log`. When a node finishes, it appends its top-50 reranked chunks to `chunks_pool` tagged with `written_by=node_id` (deduped by `chunk_id` so two nodes that hit the same page don't double-count), writes a short citation-tagged answer to `answer_log`, and extends `doc_set` with any new `doc_ids` it surfaced. The synthesizer at the end never re-retrieves — it reads only from memory.

Now three concrete plans the planner would emit for finance queries spanning your typical document universe (10-Ks, CMBS prospectuses, leveraged-buyout filings, ISDA definitions).**1 · Independent fan-out** — "Compare the leverage ratios reported in the most recent 10-K filings of Apollo, Carlyle, and KKR."

Three entities, one metric, no cross-references between the entities. The planner sees that and emits one discovery node plus three sibling query nodes with no dependencies among them. n1 calls `find_documents("Apollo Carlyle KKR latest 10-K")` and writes the resulting candidate doc_ids into `doc_set` partitioned by entity (Apollo→docs_A, Carlyle→docs_C, KKR→docs_K). n2, n3, n4 fan out the instant n1 completes — they're issued to three workers in parallel, each calling `query_documents("leverage ratio, debt-to-equity, net leverage", doc_ids=docs_X)`. Each writes its top-50 reranked chunks into `chunks_pool` tagged with its node_id, and a short citation-tagged answer into `answer_log` ("Apollo's net leverage at FYE 2024 was 1.4x per page 87 of the 10-K"). At synth time, the LLM reads three rows from `answer_log` and the union of chunks for grounding. The win here is wall-clock latency — three independent retrievals run in roughly the time of one.

**2 · Dependent chain** — "What is the weighted average life of the most senior tranche in the most recent CMBS deal sponsored by Blackstone?"

The textbook two-hop. n3 cannot be written until you know which deal "most recent" refers to. The planner emits a placeholder: `n3.subquery = "weighted average life of senior tranche of {n2.answer.deal_id}"`. The executor blocks n3 until n2's `answer_log` row is written, then substitutes. n2 itself is a real retrieval — `query_documents("most recent Blackstone-sponsored CMBS deal 2024 2025", doc_ids from n1)` — and its answer will be something like "BX Trust 2025-CHR". n3 then frequently fires its own small `find_documents` call scoped to that specific deal (prospectus, pricing supplement, FWP), expanding `doc_set` mid-execution before issuing the WAL query. This is also where replanning matters: if n2 returns an ambiguous answer ("most recent" by issuance date vs settlement date), the agent's replan hook can re-issue n2 with refined criteria rather than propagate a bad placeholder downstream.

**3 · Hybrid DAG** — "For the two largest leveraged buyouts announced in 2025, compare leverage at close to the ISDA standard credit event triggers."

Two structurally distinct branches that share nothing except the final synthesis. The LBO branch is a small dependent chain that fans out: n1 finds the top-2 LBOs (returns LBO_A, LBO_B in `answer_log`), then n2 and n3 fan out in parallel to extract leverage at close for each, each calling `query_documents` filtered to the relevant deal's S-4/8-K/credit agreement. The ISDA branch is n4, an independent root — it has no `depends_on` and the executor dispatches it at t=0 alongside n1. n4 retrieves over your ISDA definitions sub-corpus (the 2014 Credit Derivatives Definitions and any supplements). The non-obvious planner skill is recognizing that ISDA standards don't depend on which LBOs you picked; a naive planner would serialize n4 after n1 and lose the parallelism. At synth, the LLM joins `answer_log[n2, n3]` (leverage values) against `answer_log[n4]` (standard triggers) to produce the comparison.

A pattern worth noting across all three: `chunks_pool` is the grounding substrate the synthesizer sees, but `answer_log` is what carries semantic state between nodes. Keeping these separate is what lets you swap synthesis models, run coverage-rerank cheaply against the sub-questions, and verify claims chunk-by-chunk after the fact — without re-running any retrieval.
